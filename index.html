The Journey Read My Story

From Nigeria
to
Johns Hopkins

It started on Christmas Eve, 2009. Everyone was asleep. I was awake, restless, and bored, so I tried out a gift my father had bought, a View-Master and its accompanying discs. As I peered through its lenses, I was completely immersed in stunning New York scenery. For all I cared, I was no longer home. I was in New York.

That moment planted a seed. By age 13, I had set a goal: to create the world's first dynamic, persistent, wholly immersive virtual world, one that would untether the human experience from the laws of physical reality. Almost everything I have done since has been in service of that goal.

In 2016, my father had an accident. The road the government refused to fix. Our family fell. Middle class became poverty, fast. I was fifteen. So I started a retail chocolate business. Whatever it took.

At seventeen I launched Ouche, a fashion-tech startup. Managed suppliers in China, built a sales team, reached ₦2,000,000 in annual revenue. The following year I helped my brother start Odo Labs, an IT services company. I ran the business side, did hardware engineering part-time. Then in 2019, I earned a scholarship to study Computer Engineering in Turkey. I arrived and spent the first year learning Turkish before a single lecture in my field.

Then COVID arrived. Early. Before there was a vaccine. Before there was a cure. The hospitals were full. I got sick, in isolation, alone, far from home, with no treatment available and nowhere to go. The politicians had downplayed it. The streets outside my window filled with protests. I watched from quarantine, too sick to leave.

I resolved to live. I started reading, obsessively and voraciously. History of Black people. Politics. Geopolitics. I began to understand why countries are mismanaged. Why the road that broke my father was never fixed. Why I had to leave Nigeria to get an education worth having. Man's Search for Meaning helped me more than I can say.

I could not quit school. My scholarship was valid for four consecutive years only. I pushed through, sick, isolated, building and reading and surviving. I graduated on time.

I was admitted to Brown, Duke, and Johns Hopkins. Brown had the most scholarship. I chose Brown. I arrived in the summer, ready to begin graduate school. Then I was removed. Over $200 in course materials I could not pay for on time. Not a grade. Not a misconduct. Two hundred dollars. The system did not bend.

Now I hold a new admission letter. Johns Hopkins University. M.S. in Engineering Management, Innovation and Multidisciplinary Design. Fall 2026. With a $20,000 scholarship. I still need $74,243 to cover the full year.

I am not asking for charity. I am making you an offer.

II
The Blockchain

It should not come as a surprise that I am at least somewhat ambitious. I hope to build a consequential blockchain, and I do not use the word consequential lightly.

Consequential technology is technology that has intense, positive effects on the human experience. This blockchain, whose purpose I cannot yet reveal, was the subject of my final year research project. It will have a "voice", and the first few blocks will be named after those who supported me.

In every subsequent block, till eternity, your record will be embedded, indicating that you helped me pay for graduate school at a crucial moment. On the day it goes live, the Voice will reach out to you with your block location and a place to input your hash figure for a special, private message.

If not a blockchain, then on another consequential product I build. Building this blockchain remains one of my most cherished goals.

✓ Certain — if the product is built, your record is in it
III
The Possible Financial Return

I would make you an offer for an income share agreement, an ISA. But this has proven difficult to set up from where I currently live. And beyond logistics, an ISA presents a danger I have to be honest with you about.

It potentially exposes me to harm. A malicious party could make me an offer in expectation of harming me, so they can write it off as a loss on their books and gain the benefits that come with it. Insurance compensation, tax write-offs. This is not paranoia. It happens. I will not structure anything that makes my life a financial instrument someone else holds.

In offering this deal the way I have, I am trying to ensure my well-being and the certainty of great returns for those who genuinely support me at a crucial time.

What I can tell you is this: I intend to pay you back. Within approximately five years of starting work, I hope to return your donation plus 10%. Use the calculator below. I mean it. But for my safety, it is not a contract and it is not owed.
